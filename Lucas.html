Dopamine Information

“Dopamine is an organic chemical of the catecholamine and phenethylamine families.” (Conrad 2018) Dopamine plays a crucial role in our bodies controlling a multitude of factors. It plays a role in executive functions, motivation, pain and pleasure management, reinforcement, and our reward system. It does this in a healthy individual by sending “signaling cascades that are exerted via binding to dopaminergic receptors” (Conrad). This means that dopamine is used as a chemical messenger between dopaminergic neurons in the brain. It can be excitatory or inhibitory depending where in the brain and which receptor it binds to. When your brain is expecting a reward for doing something it enjoys, dopamine is released. Creating a cycle of motivation, reward and then reinforcement. A healthy person with normal dopamine levels will most likely feel alert, focused, motivated, and happy. Dopamine receptors are metabotropic and meditate their effects through G-proteins. This means the effects of dopamine are slow acting and long lasting as opposed to ionotropic receptors that are fast acting. There are 6 dopaminergic receptors known so far, these are D1, D2a, D2b, D3, D4, and D5 (Williams College Neuroscience). While there are around 86 billion neurons in the brain, only about 1 million of those are dopaminergic cells. These limited cells though are well distributed throughout 4 major tracts of the brain. There is the nigrostriatal tract beginning in the substantia nigra that aids in motor control. There is the tuberoinfundibular tract that aids in the control of hormones. There are 2 more tracts that are housed in the mesolimbocortical system, these aid in motivation, emotional behavior, and organized thought processes (Williams College Neuroscience). Dopamine is synthesized from its precursor L-DOPA in the nerve terminal where it is held until an electrical impulse called an action potential travels down the axon of the neuron to release it. This process is called exocytosis.

GABA Information

GABA is an important neurotransmitter that is integral in regulation of the brain’s communication. GABA’s main role is to inhibit activity of the neurons it binds to. In relation to dopamine, the brain uses GABA to reduce the amount of dopamine released into the nucleus accumbens. GABA is produced from glutamate in inhibitory neurons called GABAergic neurons. GABA plays a role in sleep, relieving anxiety and stress, blood pressure regulation, heart rate, brain development, pain, glucagon secretion, and immune response. It is a highly diverse and important neurotransmitter. It has both ionotropic such as the ligand gated ion channel GABAa, and metabotropic receptors such as the GABAb, throughout the central and peripheral nervous system.

Brain Regions Involved in Opioid Dependence

There are several regions of the brain known to be involved with opioid addiction. These include the ventral tegmental area (VTA), pre-frontal cortex (PFC), the basal ganglia, more specifically the nucleus accumbens (NAc), the locus ceruleus (LC) and the amygdala. The VTA and NAc make up part of what is called the mesolimbic reward system or the mesolimbic dopamine pathway, which is a series of channels in the brain that are responsible for motivation, reward, pleasure and reinforcing stimuli (Kosten, M.D. et al.). Anytime a person does something enjoyable or pleasurable, dopaminergic action potentials (AP) are sent from the ventral tegmental area (one of the primary dopamine production sites in the brain) to the nucleus accumbens. There are also the mesocortical dopamine pathways and the nigrostriatal dopamine pathways. In this essay I will be focusing on the areas of the brain associated with the mesolimbic reward system. The nucleus accumbens is a subcortical nucleus, part of the striatum and basal ganglia. It is strongly associated with reward and reinforcement. “It is here in the NAc, where dopamine primarily mediates feelings of pleasure and reward” (Bridges, 2016). The NAc also plays a crucial role in day to day activities, not just drug seeking behavior. Some of its main functions are positive reinforcement of behaviors, aversion (negative reinforcement), processing fear, and it also plays a role in slow wave sleep (non-REM). The Ventral tegmental area of the brain is made up of dopamine-containing neurons that interact strongly with the PFC and the NAc. The VTA is curial in the expression of behaviors associated with drug use. It contains the most dopaminergic neurons in the brain, which in a healthy person are regulated by inhibitory neurotransmitters (NT) such as GABA to keep regular amounts of dopamine circulating within the dopaminergic pathways. When an individual abuses opioids this inhibitory process is prevented from doing its job correctly, greatly increasing the amount of dopamine released from the VTA. After repeated abuse the brain will attempt to correct this imbalance and produce more inhibitory NTs. This forces the user to have to take more and more of the substance to feel the desired effects (Kosten, M.D. et al.). The Pre-frontal cortex (PFC) is a large part of the brain that plays too many roles to be summarized fully in this essay. But in relation to opioid abuse the PFC plays a role in executive function. This means decision making, self-control, planning and general higher cognitive function. In a healthy person the PFC controls the VTA and prevents drives or behaviors that would be unhealthy, like abusing substances that are potentially harmful. “But this feedback appears to be compromised in individuals who become addicted to drugs” (Kosten, M.D. et al.). This reduction of the PFC’s control leads to maladaptive behaviors. Examples would be drug seeking behavior, despite knowing the legal and health ramifications of illicit drug use. The locus ceruleus (LC) is a nucleus in the brain stem. Its primary role is to synthesize norepinephrine or noradrenaline (NA) “a brain chemical that stimulates wakefulness, muscle tone, and respiration, among other functions.” (Kosten, M.D. et al.). When a person is administered opioids the NA production process inside the neuron is inhibited, reducing release of NA. When abuse of the opioid such as heroin continues, the brain attempts to correct the imbalance by increasing materials needed for NA inside the LC neurons. This process results in some of the symptoms of opioid withdrawal, which will be discussed more in depth later in this essay.
